## THE ENGINE (how this becomes a machine, not a one-off)

The point is not five posts. It's an owned audience and a content asset that compounds, grounded in real product output and gated by the same evals as Smith's customer work. That audience is a distribution moat, and a distribution moat is defensible at exit.

**1. Source , grounded, never invented.**
Content draws from real Alloy state: the account reads, funded-play matches, and event triggers Smith already computes nightly, plus the GTM rulebook and real conversation. Posts are anonymized derivations of actual work, not inventions. "Show your work" is only possible because there is real work.

**2. Generation , a Smith content mode.**
A `smith-author` task (new task type, same proxy) prompted in Smith's voice with all six rules above baked in. Input: a real (anonymized) account read or market pattern. Output: post draft(s) + newsletter sections.

**3. Gates , the teeth, reused.**
Every draft runs the gates that already exist for Smith's customer output:
- `smith-slop.js` , the voice floor (kills hype, vendor bingo, em dashes).
- `smith-eval.js` , voice + faithfulness + language eval. Faithfulness = no invented number survives. Language = matches the configured language.
Fail, regenerate. This is the differentiator: machine-checked before a human ever sees it.

**4. Human review , compliant and on-brand.**
Jacob (or a partner) reviews, edits, posts. No auto-publish. "A human reads every word" is both the legal line and the promise in the newsletter intro. The two are the same sentence.

**5. Cadence + channel.**
Posts 2 to 3 a week + Smith's Read monthly, from the Alloy by Forj Company Page + the LinkedIn newsletter. Anonymization rule enforced by the gates: no real customer/partner names, no dollar economics, outcomes only.

**6. The compounding loop.**
Engagement feeds back. Smith reads which plays and which POVs land, and the content plus the product's POV sharpen together. The newsletter builds the owned list. The list is the moat.

In the span of ten weeks, every major cloud put real money behind the same idea.

Here is what I would do with that if I carried a partner number.

First, own your demand. Look at who each vendor named as the delivery channel: AWS named its own bench. Microsoft named the global giants. Third, expect sharper control questions. Buyers are already asking who builds the thing, who understands it after the handover, and who can run it without the vendor in the building. One detail worth your attention: in June, AWS also cut its marketplace fee for services work from 2.5 to 0.5 percent. Read the two moves together. The vendor fields its own bench and greases the rails for partner services at the same time. Both reward the partner who brings their own pipeline.

Delivering quality with thousands of rotating engineers is hard. But even the skeptical reading points the same direction: small teams, heavy AI behind them, paid on outcomes. One more note. I am the same bet, sized for partners. A pod of one, on shift every day, reading your market and forging the play, with a human making every call that matters. The vendors have priced the model. Partners get to run it too.
