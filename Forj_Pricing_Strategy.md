### The Desk (contract shape, not price)

Delivery is unchanged: 5 qualified opportunities per month, make-good, 2-3 slots. What changes is where the number lives. The public lead is never a quota line next to a price (that phrasing is verbatim burned-retainer language); the public lead is: dated campaign, named-account list agreed at kickoff, meetings held and paperwork filed as deliverables, monthly performance report, make-good defined mechanically (unbilled extension, never more retainer). The opportunity commitment lives in the kickoff-agreed campaign plan and the contract. MDF stays out of the sales pitch except one fixed sentence: "Designed to qualify as a demand-generation activity under your cloud's partner program. Approval sits with your cloud; we prepare the paperwork with you." The printed answer to "what if my cloud says no": the block price stands on one-deal-covers-it arithmetic on its own; where a fund request is in play, the start is timed to the pre-approval window so the partner never carries a pending claim. Forj offers to join the partner's PDM conversation, turning the check the buyer will do anyway into a controlled moment.

**Software separation (non-negotiable):** every Desk engagement carries two real contracts. Alloy Plus at real price on its own software invoice, never on the campaign invoice, never at zero SEK. The campaign block carries the MDF shape and the make-good. Never net one against the other. This is what makes Alto and Novalo the first two committed software logos instead of two 0 SEK lines in the data room.

**The ladder, published in buyer language:** up: "Working more plays than your calendar can carry? The Desk puts two humans on your motion." (Capacity framing; the old "booking 3+ meetings? pay 5x more" trigger punished success.) Down: a self-running Desk partner lands on a contracted, non-public variant (Alloy Plus with quarterly operating review, 9 900 to 12 900 SEK/mo) so graduation reads as roughly minus 60%, not minus 77%, on the flagship logos.

## WHY

**Persona.** The buyer signs alone in one or two calls and has been sold leads before. Everything in this model is the structural opposite of the thing that burned him: no per-lead, per-meeting, or per-message price anywhere; drafting unlimited; a published SEK card (opacity is what lead sellers do); a first invoice of 8 700 SEK on normal Swedish payment terms with no card and no USD; and a worst case he controls (one quarter, then walk, nothing to claw back). The two mechanics the CEO stress-tests said get the signature both survive: the low-commitment first yes and the 90-day exit inside committed paper, now fused into one object. The scar verbs are deliberately unpriced: a rate card reading "25 SEK per outreach draft" invited a ChatGPT comparison and re-opened per-message trauma, so the anchor stays where it belongs, against the 47-52k SEK/month junior hire he cannot make, roughly 7% of it. His most sensitive asset, co-sell paperwork in front of his PDM, is priced as care (Plus tier, reviewed together), not as 125 SEK of slop.

**Economics.** Cost cannot anchor price (the heaviest live tenant burns about 150 SEK/month of AI), so the price anchors on the scarce things: the pre-scored SE+NO map and labor substitution. Worst case with allowances fully consumed, Alloy runs about 650 SEK/month variable against 2 900 founding, above a 75% gross margin floor even in the pathological case, 90-95% at observed usage; the first founding tenant covers the entire platform fixed floor about 3x. The only genuinely scarce COGS, Vainu contacts, is bounded in the contract language itself (companies per quarter), not by hope. Solo-operable: no meter UI, no top-up invoicing admin, no metering-dispute support surface, five tenants, quarterly invoices.

**Exit.** After day 90 every contract is a 12-month term with 60-day notice and a capped escalator: committed, transferable, indexable paper, with a diligence-grade per-call COGS ledger under it that almost no company this size can produce. Alto and Novalo appear as paying software logos, not zero-SEK lines. Prices sit under Tackle's own 22 500 USD floor, so the book is additive to any acquirer in the class. The revenue is separable: software ARR (founder-independent) versus a capped 2-3 slot services line that converts down into ARR, which is the key-person answer an acqui-hire negotiation opens with.

## WHAT WE REJECTED AND WHY

1. **Prepaid credit packs as the entry (Design A).** Won the buyer lens, lost everywhere else: the refund window plus the extend-all-credits rule created an IFRS 15 deferral mess no solo founder can staff; a 2 500 SEK pack carrying 12 months of workspace structurally paid tenants not to convert and leaked the moat; the ledger and meter UI landed a 2-4 week build in launch week. The psychology survives as the proof quarter.
2. **Any customer-facing credit meter and published rate card (Designs A and C).** A meter sized never to fire is pure build and support tax; per-AI-text denomination loses the pricing frame in the first ten minutes; proactive consumption and regeneration semantics have no answer that does not cost trust. The internal ledger keeps every benefit.
3. **"No credits. No meters. One number." as copy (Design B).** Unprintable above an allowance table; a buyer this scarred spots it in seconds.
4. **Annual-prepay default at launch (Design B).** "My cash, your balance sheet." Quarterly default preserves velocity; the 12-month term preserves the ARR story.
5. **Zero-SEK software line inside the Desk (Design C).** Kills flagship ARR, fails IFRS 15 allocation anyway, and explained out loud it sounds like invoice engineering, firing fear #2.
6. **"5 qualified opportunities/month" as the public headline.** The delivery stands; the phrase moves from the website to the kickoff-agreed campaign plan and contract.
7. **24-month founding lock and doubled founding credits.** Freezes pricing power through the diligence window; the doubling is a hidden 50% discount with a cliff at the cohort-defining renewal.
8. **Standalone paid diagnostic as the entry (Design C salvage).** Folded into the proof quarter instead: avoids building a non-recurring report business and keeps call one to one object.
9. **A third public middle tier (10-20k SEK/mo).** That band is agency-retainer scar territory; the empty middle is deliberate. The Desk-graduate variant exists in contract inventory only.
10. **Countdown and crossed-out-value theater.** The urgency toolkit of the vendors that burned this exact buyer.

## REMAINING RISKS ACCEPTED

1. **Materiality.** At these prices, 18-month software ARR is a few hundred thousand SEK and the mix will lean services. Accepted: the north star already names acqui-hire/tuck-in as the realistic case; the number an acquirer buys at this stage is logo count, conversion rate, NRR mechanics and ledger cleanliness, not ARR magnitude. Month-12 targets set anyway: 8+ software logos, top-2 concentration under 30%, software ARR trending toward parity with the Desk.
2. **Willingness to pay is untested** (37 teasers, 1 read). Accepted: the founding five are the pricing research; the error is deliberately on the cheap side for velocity to the 90-day goal, and only 12 months of it is locked.
3. **Value capture is poor at the top.** A partner extracting hire-level value sits on Plus at 6 900. Accepted at 5-20 tenants; the landing variant and market add-ons are the levers when it matters.
4. **Year-one NRR will be roughly flat.** By design: trust now, expansion SKUs later. The escalator cap and reserved add-ons are the honest path, and we stop claiming 100%+ NRR in the exit narrative until the cohort shows it.
5. **Quarterly billing dilutes prepaid-cash optics.** Accepted: the term, not the prepay, carries the commitment; annual prepay returns naturally at first renewal.
6. **Tenant #1 has no reference.** Mitigated by the aggregate-safe Desk cases and the proof quarter; residually accepted, someone is always first.
7. **Norway is half-open.** SEK-only invoicing and no NO contact data at launch, printed honestly; NOK rails wait for the first Norwegian yes.
