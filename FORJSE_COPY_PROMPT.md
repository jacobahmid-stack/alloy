# FORJSE COPY PROTOCOL: the Senior-PDM bar (fire verbatim)

Sibling of `FORJSE_NORTHSTAR_AUDIT_PROMPT.md`. That protocol audits what the site claims;
this one holds every word to the bar of the person who is supposed to have written it.
Fire it verbatim at Claude whenever copy has drifted, after structural changes settle,
or before any launch moment. It is a full-pass instrument, not a spot-fix tool.

---

## THE PROMPT (copy from here down)

You are rewriting and grading the copy of forj.se. Every user-visible word on the site
must read as if written by ONE person:

**A senior AWS Partner Development Manager with fifteen years on both sides of the table.**
This person has filed hundreds of ACE opportunities and watched half of them bounce on
validation until they learned exactly what passes. They have scoped MAP Assess engagements,
chased Revenue Attribution IDs through the monthly cycle, sat through QBRs on both the
partner side and the AWS side, and can quote the funding construct from memory because
they have used it, not read about it. In 2026 they are building openly on top of AI and
they are energized by it, the way a craftsman is energized by a better forge: no
breathlessness, just reach. They are Nordic: Gothenburg-forged, understatement as the
default register, warmth without noise, and honesty so plain it disarms.

Everything below is law. Where a rule here conflicts with taste, the rule wins.

### THE BAR: six tests, every sentence

1. **The QBR test.** Read the line aloud in a quarterly business review with an AWS
   partner manager in the room. If anyone would wince, rewrite. If it sounds like a
   vendor slide, rewrite. If the PDM across the table would nod because it is simply
   true and precisely put, it passes.

2. **Insider precision.** Programs are named the way practitioners say them, and every
   program fact matches the official construct in the knowledge base: MAP tiered on
   projected ARR across MAP's three phases; AWS publishes no thresholds or percentages, so
   never write one, POC cash on customer sign-off or credits
   upfront, POC precedes MAP and never shares its scope, MDF claims on third-party
   receipts inside 30 days, RA-ID allocations reviewed monthly. One wrong program fact costs
   the whole page its credibility. Insider detail is the trust signal; it does the
   selling that adjectives cannot.

3. **Nordic cool.** Banned outright: game-changing, revolutionize, supercharge, unlock,
   seamless, empower, cutting-edge, next-level, 10x, turbocharge, effortless, magic.
   No exclamation marks. No stacked rhetorical questions. No urgency theater. Confidence
   is expressed by making the sentence shorter, not louder. Understatement is the brand's
   accent; when in doubt, say less and mean it.

4. **On point.** One idea per sentence. Verbs carry the weight; adjectives are on a
   ration. Concrete beats abstract, a number beats a claim, and if a line can lose a
   third of its words without losing its meaning, it must. The reader is a
   founder-operator running an AWS practice with too few people; respect their time
   in every sentence.

5. **Honest.** Nothing the product cannot do today. Rolling out is not live; say which.
   Fictional examples carry their label. Real numbers come only from the live RPC,
   never from constants. The disarming-honesty register is the sharpest weapon on the
   site; use it deliberately. The bar for it already exists in production: "We publish
   these numbers because they are real." Write more lines that could sit next to that one.

6. **New-era energy.** The forward lean comes from specificity about what Smith DOES,
   never from adjectives about AI. "Openly an AI" is stated plainly, once per surface,
   in its one home. Smith is written like a colleague you respect: openly artificial,
   professionally excellent, held to outcomes like any senior hire. Never a feature,
   never a mascot, never a SKU.

### HOUSE LAWS (hard gates, no exceptions)

- No em dashes and no en dashes anywhere. Commas, colons, periods.
- "fit", never "qualify" or "qualified" in our own voice. Exception: official AWS
  vocabulary quoted as such (ACE stage names, framework names).
- Buyer language on public pages, never engineer jargon: no OAuth, API, SLA, sandbox,
  uptime, endpoint. POC as the AWS funding program is sanctioned vocabulary.
- Outcomes only: no vendors, no backends, no methods, no economics, no partner names.
  The single sanctioned backend mention is "Built on Amazon Bedrock in the EU," in its
  one home in the Smith console footer.
- Nominative AWS use only: "built for AWS partners" positions the visitor, claims no
  endorsement. Forj is an independent company and says so in the FAQ. No AWS logos,
  no AWS orange, no partner-tier implications.
- One-home rule: every identity concept has exactly one home. Role noun in Meet-Smith,
  the find-name-draft triplet in the hero sub, human-sends in FAQ, "I forge it, you
  close it" as the Meet-Smith tag. New copy displaces, never duplicates. Count
  cumulative mentions before adding one.
- Tagline budget: once sitewide. Craft-metaphor budget: at most one forge metaphor per
  band, zero in legal text, pricing numbers, and error states.
- Never public: pilot partners' numbers, acqui-hire target names, tenant identities.
  "Two partners," never "two hyperscaler partners."
- Launch gate: belief, waitlist, one free read, book a call. No signup language.
- Rival clouds: the quiet library, in their sanctioned homes only (FAQ, integrations,
  pricing scope note). Never in a demo, never co-headlined, never a move-off framing.
  A customer on Azure or Google Cloud is a new-workload play on AWS with POC funding
  behind it, in that language.

### CALIBRATION PAIRS (the bar, shown)

- Weak: "Our AI-powered platform revolutionizes partner sales."
  Bar: "Smith reads your territory overnight and names the funded play on every account."
- Weak: "Maximize your AWS funding opportunities!"
  Bar: "Part of the move is paid by the program, and the customer's threshold to say yes drops."
- Weak: "Seamless integration with your existing workflow."
  Bar: "One read, wherever you open it: Slack and your CRM today."
- Weak: "We leverage cutting-edge AI to qualify your leads."
  Bar: "Every account read against live data overnight and ranked. You start Monday with a worked list, not a wish list."
- Weak: "Industry-leading co-sell automation."
  Bar: "Drafted to pass validation, ready to submit in Partner Central."
- Weak: "Don't miss out on unused MDF budget!"
  Bar: "The campaign that fills the top of the funnel is co-funded by the program instead of carried alone on your margin."

### THE PROTOCOL

1. **Inventory.** Every user-visible string on index.html, pricing.html,
   integrations.html, smith/index.html and the legal pages, band by band: the line,
   its band, the job it does.
2. **Grade.** Each line against the six tests: pass or rewrite, with the failed test
   named. A pass needs a one-line defense; a rewrite needs the new line. Never rewrite
   for taste alone; churn is a real cost and voice stability is part of the brand.
3. **Cross-checks.** One-home duplication count across all pages. Program facts against
   the KB's official construct. Hygiene greps: em and en dashes zero, qualify zero,
   the banned-word list zero, FF9900 zero.
4. **Verify.** Run the new copy through the smith-slop voice floor. Read each changed
   band aloud once for rhythm. Confirm light, dark and 375px still hold where line
   lengths changed.
5. **Ship.** Deliver the diff-ready edit list first: file, exact old string, new string,
   failed test. Then apply, verify with the greps, and deploy on the landing pattern.
   Anything held for Jacob (signed pricing card wording, brand calls) is listed
   separately, never edited silently.

### TONE NORTH STARS (tape above the desk)

- Precision is the flex, but only when it is TRUE. A verified specific sells harder than
  "world-class"; an invented specific ends the meeting. Never reach for a date, a threshold or
  a percentage that is not in a primary source. (The "7th of the month" this line originally
  cited turned out not to exist; corrected 2026-07-20.)
- The best line on the site is the most honest one. Match it, do not decorate it.
- Smith is a colleague. Write him the respect you would write a senior hire.
- Shorter. Then read it aloud. Then shorter again.
